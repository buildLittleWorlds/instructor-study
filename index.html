The Instructor's Study

Daniel — one paper-app per week, built in public, alongside the class
The project: The False Mean — Can You Tell Excellence from Polish? A twelve-week study of prototypes, randomness, and taste in language-model writing, conducted as a series of small deployed paper-apps. Each one is a complete argument you can read and test.

Course boundary: this is the instructor's advanced project and possibility space. Student paper-apps begin with curiosity, one interaction, and an explanation; they do not have to copy this study's ratings, datasets, or research structure.

 Session 1 · Embeddings and prototypes

The Map of Meaning

A real neural network draws a map of words — and places yours on it. Roam the geography and find the prototype at a cluster's center.

 Session 2 · Probability and temperature

Where Does Randomness Enter?

Restart the same Game of Life seed, randomize its beginning, then add a noisy update. From there, move to the dice and sentence garden to see what temperature changes—and what it does not.

 Session 3 · Attention and context

Attention Spotlight

Change the visible context, guess which words settle an ambiguous meaning, and reveal the instructor's hand-authored teaching cues.

 Session 4 · Pretraining, RLHF & slop

The Slop Detector

Predict, then measure: a model's answers to eight unrelated prompts turn out ~49× more alike than eight humans'. Click each tell to see the evidence.

 Session 5 · From surprise to research question · the hinge

Question Zero

Kill-test seven research questions, watch one survive, then meet the three doors — the false mean, the random swerve, and the true swerve — that lock my whole study.

 Session 6 · Evaluation, criteria & agreement

The Agreement Machine

Rate three openings on gut, then with a rubric, and watch your agreement with the panel leap from chaos to near-total — the moment taste turns into evidence.

 Session 7 · Evidence at scale & honest logging

The Run

Explore the whole dataset — every prompt, all three conditions, scored the same way. Try to "prove" anything with one row, then seal your bet before the averages.

 Session 8 · Analysis & the honest average

The Reveal

Open the sealed envelopes, then the averages Session 7 refused to compute — and take the wheel: eighty analysis paths, three outlier toggles, and the one honest path where the winner flips.

 Session 9 · Writing the argument

The Four Corners

The study's whole claim, redrawn as a map: sort eight unlabeled passages into the false mean, the random swerve, classic form, or the true swerve — then see where the room disagreed with you.

 Session 10 · Judging the judge & authorship

The Second Opinion

An AI judge scores my study with my own rubric. Crown its winner before you look, side with a verdict blind — and see exactly which criteria a machine can check, and which stay human.
