The Agreement Machine

Thesis. "Quality" sounds hopelessly personal — until you notice that trained judges, given clear criteria, start to agree. That agreement is the whole trick: it turns a feeling into evidence. This page is a small machine for watching it happen — to you.

Three meanings of "it works"

The rubric above sharpens judgment. This bench sharpens the other half of evaluation: which behavior you test. Below is Bill Gosper's glider gun — the 1970 machine that answered Conway's $50 wager by firing a fresh glider every 30 generations, forever. Watch it run, then put the same system through three different meanings of "it works."

Generation 0 · the gun waits.

1 · Reach the target onceDoes a glider cross into the far field within 50 generations?
2 · Keep workingIs it still firing around generation 150?
3 · Recover from damageDamage the board first, then: is it still firing long after your hit?

All three verdicts are computed by silently running the real board and checking the far field (the lower-right region a fired glider must cross). Tests 1 and 2 restart from the intact gun; test 3 runs from whatever you left on the board. The board is finite; gliders that reach the edge are absorbed — which is exactly why "still firing long after the hit" is the honest recovery check: it waits out the gliders that were already in flight when you struck.

Same system, three verdicts. The gun almost always passes 1 and 2 and fails 3 — one clumsy thumbprint and the factory unravels, because nothing ever trained it to survive you. The Growing Neural Cellular Automata paper makes exactly this move at training time: models trained only to grow may decay or explode; trained to persist, they hold; trained with damage, they regrow limbs they never lost before. Robustness isn't a property systems have by default — it's a property something put there. Watch the trained versions recover: Distill 2020 · the 2026 sequel.

The yardstick makes the jump

One more honesty tool, one research argument up from the rubric: sometimes the instrument manufactures the story. A famous debate about language models asks whether abilities "emerge" suddenly as models grow. The mirage argument: score with an all-or-nothing yardstick and ability seems to snap into existence; score the same system with partial credit and it grows smoothly. Here is that whole argument in one slider — ten-digit addition, the debate's own example.

All-or-nothing (whole sum right, p¹⁰):
Partial credit (average digits right, p):

Where the "jump" lives. The skill line is smooth by construction — nothing in the system ever jumps. Only the all-or-nothing curve has a cliff, and the cliff is pure arithmetic: p¹⁰ stays near zero until p is large, then soars. Whether an ability "emerges" can depend on the instrument (Schaeffer, Miranda & Koyejo 2023, answering Wei et al. 2022 — and the argument is live, not settled). Your four criteria above are instruments too: that is why this page shows you your least-settled criterion instead of pretending the rubric is a ruler of atoms.

What's computed: both curves are exact arithmetic from the slider (nothing simulated); the graded sum below the slider is a real random draw at your chosen skill — reroll it and watch all-or-nothing swing while partial credit barely moves.

Method

Loading…

The gun bench. Real bounded Conway board (60×40), the standard Gosper gun. Reach = any live cell in the far field (the region a fired glider must cross) within 50 generations of the intact gun; keep-working = far field still occupied when sampled around generation 140–160; recover = far field still occupied 140–200 generations after your damage, from the damaged board — late enough that gliders already in flight have been absorbed and only a working gun could still be supplying new ones.

The yardstick. All-or-nothing = p¹⁰; partial credit = p. The sample sum draws ten digits independently right with probability p. The thresholds and the framing are mine; the arithmetic is the arithmetic.

Interpretation

Same openings, same raters — the only thing that changed between the rounds was having words for what we were looking for. On gut feeling, the panel scattered from 1 to 5 on the same sentence; with the rubric, they converged. That convergence is what lets a judgment about writing count as evidence instead of just a vibe. And notice the second gift: the trained panel didn't just agree, it agreed on different scores for different openings — low for the cliché, high for the pigeon — so the criteria don't flatten everything, they discriminate. This is exactly how real AI systems are evaluated: break the fuzzy judgment into named criteria, rate blind, and check that independent judges land in the same place.

Limitation. Rating the same items twice means Round 2 is nudged by having already seen them — a cleaner test would use two separate trained-and-untrained groups on fresh items (which is what the real panels here do). And "agreement" isn't "correct": a whole panel can agree and still be wrong. Agreement makes a judgment usable as evidence, not automatically true.

Try it yourself

Pick one behavior in an app you care about. Write two different success checks for it — for example, "responds quickly" and "explains itself clearly." Test both. Notice how the yardstick changes what you decide to improve.

A success check does not need to become a class rating system. Its job is to help a builder decide what to test and what to improve next. And when a check fails — when the gun is in pieces on the floor — the next skill is the loop that fixes it. That is next session's bench.
