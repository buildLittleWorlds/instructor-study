The Map of Meaning

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. A language model turns every word into a point in a vast space of numbers, where distance means similarity of meaning. If that's true, meaning has a geography — and the "typical" member of any group sits near the center of its neighborhood. That center is a prototype, and it's where the model's default answers come from.

This week's question: can we actually see meaning as a map — and find the prototype sitting at the middle of a cluster?

Interpretation

The map isn't decoration — it's the model's actual sense of meaning, flattened from 384 dimensions down to two so our eyes can read it. Animals land near animals, feelings near feelings, without anyone telling the model what a category is; it learned the neighborhoods from how words are used. And inside each neighborhood there's a center of gravity. The word closest to that center is the most "average" member — the prototype. When you later ask a model for "an animal" or "a beautiful room," this is where its first answer comes from: the middle of the map.

Limitation. Squashing 384 dimensions into 2 loses a lot — two dots that look close on this flat map might be farther apart in the real space, and the two axes are just one choice of many. This is a faithful sketch of the model's meaning-space, not a perfect photograph. It shows that meaning has structure; it doesn't capture all of it.

Try it yourself

Put in a word that lives between two worlds — "bat" (animal or baseball?), "spring," "rock," your own name. Where does the model place it, and whose neighbor does it become? Then find a word you think the model will get wrong, and see if it does.

The model hands us the center of the map every time. So here's the whole rest of the course in one question: is the center any good? (Next week we grab a dial that pulls answers away from it.)
