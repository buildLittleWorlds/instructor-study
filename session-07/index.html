The Debug Loop

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning

What made me curious

Last week I had a bug I could not fix by wanting it fixed. A little spaceship in a Game of Life world kept dying halfway across the board, and no amount of staring at the crash site changed anything — because the crash is not a thing in the program. It is a consequence. In an emergent system you cannot edit the outcome; you can only edit the layers that produce it, then rerun, then watch. Somewhere around the fourth rerun I realized this is exactly the posture that directing an AI demands, stopped being annoyed, and started building this page.

What to notice

Session 7's idea is small and load-bearing: useful AI development is a loop — observe, describe, request, test, revise. Not a wish; a loop. The bench below holds a real bug in a real (tiny) universe, and the page will refuse to let you fix it the way everyone first tries to fix software: by demanding the result. Describe the behavior you got, then change one layer you control.

The second module then asks the quieter question underneath the loop: why does it have to run at all? Why can't anyone — you, me, a model — just see the answer from the start? That question opens the last of the Session 2 lab's three design dials: Session 3 opened neighborhood, Session 4 opened rule, and today is iteration — the unbounded update loop versus the fixed-depth forward pass. It ends at why modern AI thinks out loud.

You can't paint the outcome

This is a bounded Conway board — B3/S23, 46×18 cells, edges simply dead. The seed holds a lightweight spaceship aimed east and, sitting in its flight lane, a 2×2 block somebody left behind (fine: I left it). The goal chip states the test in public: the ship reaches the right edge — any live cell in the tinted last three columns, within 200 generations of pressing Run. Press Run and watch what actually happens.

The loop (tape it above your desk)
  1. Observe — run it; watch what actually happens.
  2. Describe precisely — when, where, what.
  3. Change one layer you control.
  4. Rerun the same test.
  5. Keep it or revert it.
The same loop, aimed at AI: you cannot edit the model's weights — nobody can, not even its makers, not directly. Your controllable layers are the prompt, the context you provide, the code you accept or reject, and the test you run. Same loop, same discipline.

Not a layer you control · the trap, included honestly

Paint the outcome

Here is the tool everyone secretly wants: a brush that paints the result. Select it and paint amber cells where you wish the ship were — at the right edge, say, arriving in style. The paint lands on a translucent overlay. Then press Step and watch the goal chip.

You painted on the glass, not in the world. The rule never reads the amber layer; the world moved underneath your paint and the goal chip did not blink. The rule only keeps what the rule can reach. To change the outcome, change a layer the rule reads: the seed, or the rule itself.

No shortcut: steps are the price

The bench taught the loop. This module is about why there is a loop at all — why nobody, human or machine, gets to skip to the end. Three small worlds, each with one cell ringed in amber, one question each: will the ringed cell be alive at generation 8? You answer twice, in order. First instantly, from the seed alone — a pure guess, and the page records it. Then step the world there and answer again.

Scorecard — answer all three worlds both ways and this box will do the arithmetic.

The gap you just felt is not a personal failing; it is load-bearing mathematics. Life's entire physics is one sentence long, and still the general question "what will this pattern eventually do?" is provably undecidable — no formula answers it for every pattern. Even for a definite horizon like our generation 8, there is usually nothing smarter than running the world. Stephen Wolfram's name for this is computational irreducibility: for most systems that compute, the only fully general method is to run it and watch. The Skip button knew this. It didn't skip the steps; it ran them faster.

The other side of dial #3. The cellular automaton on this page runs as many generations as the pattern needs: your 8 steps just now; the R-pentomino's famous 1,103 before it settles (on an unbounded board); longer if it takes longer. A transformer's depth is decided at training time and spent identically on every token. The CA's budget follows the problem; the transformer's budget is fixed before the problem arrives.
The modern coda. So what do you do when the question needs more steps than the stack has? You write. "Think step by step" — and today's reasoning models — work by generating intermediate tokens, and every generated token is one more full pass through the stack. The model is buying itself generations and paying in words. Theory agrees: transformers forced to answer immediately provably cannot solve some easy problems, and allowing intermediate steps lifts them (Merrill & Sabharwal, ICLR 2024).

Thinking tokens are generations.

Handle the analogy by the right end. "Thinking tokens are generations" is a claim about compute budget — steps of work, and where a system gets them — not about mechanism. A language model is not running a cellular automaton, a token is not a grid, and a transformer is not a CA. What matches is only this: some questions cost steps, and a system must get its steps from somewhere.

How this page was made

Computed live: every cell on the bench and in the three puzzle worlds comes from one step function — a double-buffered Uint8Array sweep with bounded, dead edges — running in this page as you press. The goal chip, the verdicts, the flicker counts, and the scorecard are read off the running state; nothing dynamical is pre-recorded. The amber wish layer is stored outside the state array and is never read by the step function — that is the trap's entire honesty.

Authored: the scenario (the ship's coordinates, the block, the goal's wording), the three candidate bug descriptions and which one unlocks, the rule notes, the three puzzle seeds and ring placements, and the layer-stack schematic. All of it sits in one marked block — EDIT HERE — at the top of this page's script.

Verified before written: every number in my prose — extinction at 43, arrival at 71, HighLife's 41, B2's wrong-reason green near 16, and the three generation-8 truths — was simulated first and typed second. The README carries the check recipe; if you edit a seed, rerun it before trusting any sentence with a number in it.

Accuracy notes, so the page can't oversell itself. (1) The layer-stack panel is a schematic: no measured activations, illustrative layer count. (2) The bench's board is finite and its edges absorb; the ship's "arrival" ends in a 4-cell remnant that is a boundary artifact, not Life physics on an infinite plane — which is fine, because arrival, not afterlife, is what the goal tests. (3) B36/S23 (HighLife) and B2/S23 are named variant rules, not Conway's Game of Life; press a rule chip and you are in a different universe. (4) The numbers in my prose describe the shipped seed. Edit the seed and the prose no longer applies — the chip stays honest because it is computed; prose can't be.

What I would try next

Swap the describe-gate chips for a free-text box and let the room judge precision out loud — the chips are training wheels, and the judging is the skill. Add a second block placed so the obvious fix passes the arrival test but fails a new one: regression, the loop's next lesson. And let the three puzzle worlds run past generation 8 with a bet on when each goes still — I have deliberately not looked, which is rather the point.

What I chose and how AI helped

AI helped with: drafting the page's code and copy under my direction, proposing candidate puzzle seeds, and running the verification simulations whose outputs my captions quote (recipe in the session README).

I chose: the bug — a ship and a block that annihilate mutually, because in Life a collision costs both parties; the goal and its public wording; which description counts as precise and why the other two stay locked; the three layers and their order — seed, then rule, then the trap; and the standing rule that no dynamical number enters my prose unless a simulation produced it first.

The fix works and I can say why. Next: the page has to say it — what the build showed, what it didn't, in words a stranger believes. That's the paper layer.
