Interpretation

Scroll it sorted by total and you'll feel the temptation immediately: pick one dazzling row and you've "proven" your favorite condition. That's exactly why a single example is worthless as evidence — with two dozen rows I can argue any story I like. The discipline is to keep all of them, rate them the same way, and refuse to conclude until every row is counted. Notice too the flagged row: halfway through I typed the wrong subject. I didn't delete it and quietly move on — I logged it, left it visible, and excluded it from the analysis. Honest logging is what separates evidence from a highlight reel.

Limitation. This is a small run with authored, illustrative text — a real study needs more prompts, more raters, and outputs actually generated by the model. And a dataset this size is fragile: one lucky row can tilt a small condition. Averaging carefully, and watching what a single outlier does to the result, is next week's whole job.

Try it yourself

Take any question you're testing and resist the urge to keep your three best examples. Collect everything — the boring, the broken, the ones that went sideways — and log the mistakes on purpose. A dataset you can trust is one where you didn't get to choose what stayed.

The rows are in. My prediction is sealed, and so is yours. Next week we do the one thing this page refused to do: compute the averages, and see whether the true swerve actually won. (And what a single outlier can do to the answer.)
