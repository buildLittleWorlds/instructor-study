The Slop Detector

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. After a model learns language, it's tuned toward answers people preferred — a process called RLHF. A documented side effect is mode collapse: the outputs get more and more alike, no matter how different the questions. The field's word for the result is consensus slop — fluent, polished, and eerily the same. If it's real, we should be able to measure it.

Course boundary: this is an instructor demonstration. It uses an illustrative sample to make learned repetition visible. It does not collect, export, or submit visitor input.

This week's question: are a model's answers to eight unrelated prompts measurably more alike than eight humans' answers to the same prompts?

The evidence underneath

Who writes the rules? The Rule Forge

The slop above is a learned behavior — nobody typed it. To feel what that means, hold it against the other way rules come to exist. Conway's Game of Life runs on B3/S23: one legible sentence a person wrote. But here is the part worth savoring: Conway didn't decree that sentence — he searched for it, for roughly two years, on Go boards and graph paper, tuning birth and survival conditions against three desiderata: a good universe shouldn't fizzle out, shouldn't blow up, and should stay surprising longer than it has any right to. This bench hands you his search.

Search that ends in a sentence. Feel how few rules light all three lamps — that's the knife's edge Conway spent two years finding by hand. His search ended in a sentence you can verify by counting neighbors. Gradient descent runs the same kind of search — try, judge against what's rewarded, adjust — and ends in a hundred billion numbers nobody can read. The deep split isn't written-versus-learned; it's search that ends in a sentence versus search that ends in numbers.
Why the big searches are learned, not written. Notice what your hands are doing: flipping whole rule-bits, blind, one guess at a time. A rule table has no slope — there is no "warmer" between B2 and B3, only different universes. A continuous space of numbers does have slopes, and that's precisely why models are trained rather than authored: gradient descent needs somewhere to roll. (And even then it's hard — a 2020 paper found tiny networks that provably can represent Life's rule still "rarely converge" when trained to learn it: Springer & Kenyon. Representable is not learnable.)

Guardrail: a transformer is not a cellular automaton, and B3/S23-style rule bits are nothing like weights. The bench shows one honest thing: what kind of object each search produces — a sentence, or a mountain of numbers.

Method

Loading…

The Rule Forge. Real cellular automaton, 32×22 bounded board, R-pentomino seed. Changing the rule triggers a silent 200-step run that computes three verdicts: fizzle = population hits zero (the step is reported); blow up = the board ever exceeds 35% full, or the last twenty steps still churn more than 60 cell-changes per step; quietly alive = neither of those, at least 10 cells at step 200, still changing at least 3 cells per step. The thresholds are mine — that's true to the story, since Conway's desiderata were his.

Fine skin. One state array, two canvases: hard squares, and soft radial brushes over the same twenty-by-twenty numbers. Nothing about the simulation changes.

The reward model. Twelve authored answers in six pairs, each tagged with its ingredients (stock phrasing, hedging, list shape, length, concrete detail, a risk taken, warmth, plainness). The page counts the tags of what you chose against what you rejected and assembles a sample answer from your top-rewarded ingredients. Nothing is collected or sent — the tally lives and dies in this tab.

Interpretation

The two bars usually aren't close. The AI answers share a large slice of their vocabulary with one another even though the prompts have nothing in common — a sunset, a study tip, a pep talk all reach for journey, ultimately, it's important to remember, truly. The human notes barely overlap at all, because each person grabbed something concrete and specific: a spilled coffee, a traffic-cone orange, a cat. That gap is consensus slop made visible. And it raises the real question behind the mechanism: the model sounds this way because it was tuned toward what raters rewarded — so whose preferences, and what did they reward, to sand every answer down to the same shape?

Limitation. Sameness here is measured by shared vocabulary — a rough, surface proxy. Two texts can share few words yet still feel identical in rhythm and stance, which this misses; a deeper version would compare meaning with embeddings, not words. The AI answers are also longer than the human notes, which inflates overlap — that's why the control bar exists (it stays low, so length isn't the story). And the texts are a small, authored sample. The direction is real and repeatable; the exact percentages are just this sample's.

Try it yourself

Give your AI chat five prompts that have nothing to do with each other and read the answers back to back. Hunt for the phrase that shows up in all five — that's your model's tell. Then ask it to "avoid clichés and be original," and watch what new repeated phrase appears instead.

The obvious fix is "make it weirder." But if you just crank the strangeness, you may get a different sameness — edgy slop instead of safe slop. The useful studio question is smaller: what behavior did your prompt encourage, and what one change could make it more intentional? A behavior you've shaped on purpose is the seed of a direction — and which curiosity deserves a whole direction is next session's question.
