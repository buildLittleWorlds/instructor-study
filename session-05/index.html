The Next Move

What made me curious

The Game of Life's early history reads like a lab notebook of wonderings. Conway wondered whether one simple rule could keep a board interesting. His group wondered what five particular cells would do. Conway wondered — publicly, with money on it — whether anything could grow forever. Some of those curiosities became directions, and one became a field. I wanted to know what sorted them: why some wonderings earned weeks of graph paper while others got a shrug. Because that sorting is exactly the choice in front of my students this week — what to build next.

What to notice

This course keeps two habits going at once. When the topic is mechanism, Life is a counter-model — a small machine to hold against the large one so their differences stay sharp. Today the topic is method, and Life plays the other part: continuity. What carried Conway's group from a strange scrap of cells to a new field was never a formal hypothesis with conditions and controls. It was a chain of curiosities that each suggested an action — watch this one longer, wager on that one, build the thing that settles it. Questions can guide making without becoming hypotheses. The test is simpler and harsher: A useful curiosity gives you a next move. Watch, on this page, for the difference between wonderings that hand you an action and wonderings that just sit there sounding deep.

Course boundary: this page is an instructor demonstration — possibility space, not a checklist. You will predict and pick as you read, but only to sharpen your own eye: nothing is collected, exported, or submitted.

The five cells nobody could predict

In 1970 computer time was scarce, so Conway's group at Cambridge tracked patterns by hand — Go stones, graph paper, patience. The R-pentomino is five cells you could cover with a thumbnail, and it is famous for one reason: nobody could say what it would do. Here it is, mid-board, under the standard rule (B3/S23 — a dead cell with exactly three live neighbors is born; a live cell with two or three survives). House rule first.

Does it give you a next move?

History is the warm-up; triage is the skill. Below are four curiosities of the kind this studio actually produces, and four verdicts to file them under: watchable now (run it and look, no apparatus), buildable smaller (a real direction whose first version is much smaller than the wish), just an opinion (as stated) (no move until it's narrowed), needs a yardstick first (the move exists but hides a measurement you haven't chosen). Vote before anyone clicks. Then click your verdict — and the rivals: each chip shows the future that filing buys you, and my own filing carries a flag. Disagreement is fine; the four categories are the tool.

The seed is a knob

Card 4 filed as watchable now; here is the watching. This page is my app, and the five-cell seed is a knob I chose almost without thinking — Conway's famous scrap instead of anything else. Your letters are a different setting of the same knob. Say out loud what you expect — burns out, settles into ash, still going at five thousand — then run it. Guessing wrong is the good outcome: if you already knew, there would be nothing to find out.

The stamp drops your letters onto the board above as live cells — same rule, same Run, Fast, and Skip. Typed letters become cells and are forgotten: nothing is collected, saved, or sent.

If all three finish easily, you have a direction. If one of them sticks, that one is your week. And the third sentence is exactly what the Bench turns into a build prompt — out of your answers, nobody else's.

Method

Computed, live, in your browser: every generation of the board — a real B3/S23 engine on a 120×80 grid, edges dead; the settle check, which compares each state with the board two generations back; the glider census — exact template matching against sixteen phase-orientations the page generates in code at load time; the sparkline; and the letter stamp, which maps typed letters through a small pixel font to live cells and does nothing else. Authored by me: the three history beats, the four curiosity cards and their sixteen filings, and the pixel font's letterforms — all in one clearly marked block near the top of this page's script (search the source for EDIT HERE). One discipline throughout: the readouts always show this board's own numbers; the canonical infinite-board record appears only as quoted history, never piped into a readout. And one promise: typed letters become cells and are forgotten — nothing you type is collected, saved, or sent.

Accuracy notes. Four limits and a guardrail. The board is bounded: 120×80 with dead edges, so travelers are quietly absorbed where infinite Life — the ideal object all the canonical numbers describe — would let them fly forever. The census is narrow: the counter knows exactly the standard glider's sixteen phase-orientations; a rarer spaceship would sail past uncounted, so “gliders in flight” means gliders, not all traffic. The determinism is total: same seed, same rule, no noise — whatever surprises you here, randomness did none of it. “Settled” means period ≤ 2: a rarer oscillator with a longer beat — your letters could plausibly make one — would honestly read as “still going,” forever. And the standing guardrail: a transformer is not a cellular automaton, and this page makes no claims about LLM mechanisms. What it teaches is a continuity — how a curiosity becomes a direction — which is a fact about research and researchers, not about the innards of either machine.

I picked a direction worth one more week. The harder question arrives with it: when I ship the next version, what exactly would make me say it works? That is next session's machine.
