What made me curious

The Game of Life's early history reads like a lab notebook of wonderings. Conway wondered whether one simple rule could keep a board interesting. His group wondered what five particular cells would do. Conway wondered — publicly, with money on it — whether anything could grow forever. Some of those curiosities became directions, and one became a field. I wanted to know what sorted them: why some wonderings earned weeks of graph paper while others got a shrug. Because that sorting is exactly the choice in front of my students this week — what to build next.

What to notice

This course keeps two habits going at once. When the topic is mechanism, Life is a counter-model — a small machine to hold against the large one so their differences stay sharp. Today the topic is method, and Life plays the other part: continuity. What carried Conway's group from a strange scrap of cells to a new field was never a formal hypothesis with conditions and controls. It was a chain of curiosities that each suggested an action — watch this one longer, wager on that one, build the thing that settles it. Questions can guide making without becoming hypotheses. The test is simpler and harsher: A useful curiosity gives you a next move. Watch, on this page, for the difference between wonderings that hand you an action and wonderings that just sit there sounding deep.

Course boundary: this page is an instructor demonstration — possibility space, not a checklist. You will predict and pick as you read, but only to sharpen your own eye: nothing is collected, exported, or submitted.

The five cells nobody could predict

In 1970 computer time was scarce, so Conway's group at Cambridge tracked patterns by hand — Go stones, graph paper, patience. The R-pentomino is five cells you could cover with a thumbnail, and it is famous for one reason: nobody could say what it would do. Here it is, mid-board, under the standard rule (B3/S23 — a dead cell with exactly three live neighbors is born; a live cell with two or three survives). House rule first.

Does it give you a next move?

History is the warm-up; triage is the skill. Below are seven curiosities of the kind this studio actually produces — a couple of them are mine. For each one, make your call before you see mine: not “is it interesting?” but “what would you do with it tomorrow?” The four verdicts are the tool: watchable now (run it and look, no apparatus), buildable smaller (a real direction whose first version is much smaller than the wish), just an opinion (as stated) (no move until it's narrowed), needs a yardstick first (the move exists but hides a measurement you haven't chosen).

The study I stopped building

How this page was made

Computed, live, in your browser: every generation of the board — a real B3/S23 engine on a 120×80 grid, double-buffered, edges dead. The settle detector hashes each new state and compares it with the state two generations back (first checked only after generation 10, and a hash match is confirmed cell-by-cell before the page will say “settled”). The glider census builds its own templates at load time: it drops a lone glider on a scratch grid, runs the engine four steps to get the four phases, then takes every rotation and reflection — sixteen distinct patterns, all landing in 3×3 boxes — and counts exact, non-overlapping matches ringed by an empty one-cell border, every ten generations. The sparkline and the triage tally are computed too.

Authored by me: the three history beats, the seven curiosity cards and their verdicts, and the strand cards in Module 5C. All of that sits in one clearly marked block near the top of this page's script — search the source for EDIT HERE. The essay prose around the modules is ordinary HTML just above it. One discipline throughout: the readouts always show this board's own numbers; the canonical infinite-board record appears only as quoted history, never piped into a readout.

Accuracy notes. Three limits and a guardrail. The board is bounded: 120×80 with dead edges, so travelers are quietly absorbed where infinite Life — the ideal object all the canonical numbers describe — would let them fly forever. This run's settle numbers land close to the record only because the board is big enough for the ash to form untouched in the middle while the edges swallow the gliders. The census is narrow: the counter knows exactly the standard glider's sixteen phase-orientations; a rarer spaceship would sail past uncounted, so “gliders in flight” means gliders, not all traffic. The determinism is total: same seed, same rule, no noise — the same movie every time you load this page; whatever surprises you here, randomness did none of it. And the standing guardrail: a transformer is not a cellular automaton, and this page makes no claims about LLM mechanisms. What it teaches is a continuity — how a curiosity becomes a direction — which is a fact about research and researchers, not about the innards of either machine.

What I would try next

Let the reader draw a seed: click cells onto an empty board, then run — card 2's initials experiment, playable here instead of merely promised. And teach the census a second citizen: generate the lightweight spaceship's templates the same way the glider's are made, so the counter stops being blind to rarer travelers.

What I chose and how AI helped

AI helped with: drafting this page's code and modules from my written plan — the engine, the settle detector, the template-matching census, the card machinery — and tightening prose I supplied.

I chose: the three history beats and what each is for (a watchable seed, a public wager, the machine that answered it); the seven curiosity cards, their verdicts, and the reasoning under each; and the framing — that this session teaches triage rather than hypothesis-writing, and that the honest unit of progress is a next move.

I picked a direction worth one more week. The harder question arrives with it: when I ship the next version, what exactly would make me say it works? That is next session's machine.
