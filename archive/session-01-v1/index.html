The Prototype Problem

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. Human perception and language models both anchor on prototypes — the statistical center of the examples they have absorbed. Before we can ask whether AI writing is any good, we have to meet the average it keeps returning to.

This week's question: do people and language models share the same starting point — a built-in pull toward the prototype?

Interpretation

Two very different systems, one behavior. Infants — with no training data we can inspect — look longer at faces near the attractive prototype, and the preference collapses when the structure is disrupted. A language model — which is, mathematically, a map of averages — reaches for the same windows, plants, and neutral tones no matter how many times we ask. In embedding terms: a prototype is a centroid, and the model's default answer lives near the centroid of everything it learned about "beautiful rooms."

Limitation. Ten runs, one model, one prompt — a hint, not a proof. And the infant study concerns face perception, not room descriptions: the connection between the two is an analogy about anchoring, not an identity. Nothing here yet says whether the prototype is good, bad, or merely the starting point.

Try it yourself

Open the AI chat you already use. Ask it — in three separate conversations — for “a beautiful room” (or a beautiful anything you care about: game level, sneaker, song). Count what repeats. Then try, politely, to escape the default: “make it unusual.” Watch what it does instead.

Babies like the prototype. The model is a prototype machine. So — is the average therefore good? (Next week: what happens when we force the model away from its center.)
