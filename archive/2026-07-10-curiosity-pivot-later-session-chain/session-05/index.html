Thesis. A surprise is not a research question — it's the raw material for one. The whole craft of research is narrowing: turning "AI writing feels off" into something small enough to actually test, and interesting enough to be worth testing. A good question is one you can measure, get data for, and be surprised by — either way it turns out.

Interpretation

Narrowing feels like giving up ambition. It's the opposite. A giant question ("is AI creative?") can never be answered, so it teaches you nothing; a small one ("do readers rate a directed break above the default and a random one?") can be run in a few weeks and will change your mind about something real. The three conditions give the study its shape: the false mean (the safe default), the random swerve (difference by noise), and the true swerve (a break made on purpose). The bet is that the true swerve wins — but the reason to make the bet is that I learn something whether it does or not.

Limitation. A locked question can still be the wrong one — too small to matter, or resting on a distinction ("directed" vs. "random") that readers can't actually feel. I won't know until I build the instrument and run it. Locking the question isn't the end of doubt; it's the start of a test.

Try it yourself

Bring three of your own surprises from the last few weeks. Run each through the three kill-tests: can you measure it, can you get the data, would either answer surprise you? Keep the one that survives — that's your Question Zero, and it's worth more than ten big ideas you can't test.

To test my bet fairly I can't just ask "which is best?" — that's an opinion again. I need criteria a stranger could be trained to apply, and agree on. (Next week you become my expert panel.)
