The Naming of Things

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning

What made me curious

I watched the same Life run twice — once knowing the names of things, once not — and saw different amounts. Same board, same pixels, more world. If a handful of names can change how much a person sees, then the words on a paper-app aren't packaging under the demo. They're optics.

What to notice

Every paper-app in this studio is half machine, half caption: one working interaction plus a short, honest explanation. It is tempting to treat the words as the boring part. This page argues the opposite: explanation is part of the interface. In the lab below, the names are literally a control — a switch that changes what a reader can see without changing one pixel of what happens.

And the words are also where a page can quietly start to lie. Every sentence you write about your app makes one of three calls: this page showed it, a source I can cite shows it, or this page can't show it. Builders who know which call each sentence makes write smaller sentences that stand up harder. Not the humble version of the claim — the checkable one.

Course boundary: this is an instructor demonstration for the classroom screen. Soups, censuses, predictions, and sorted claims live in this page's memory and vanish on reload — nothing is collected, exported, or submitted.

Names on, names off

A real bounded Game of Life board, 48×32 — the same B3/S23 universe as our Session 2 lab. Stir a soup and about 38% of the middle region lights up at random; the chaos burns down to ash: still lifes and small oscillators. Then the switch. With names off, the ash is anonymous debris. With names on, a field guide of eleven shapes reads the board and takes a census. The interaction lets the reader see it; the paper layer helps them know what they saw.

The same four sentences, two ways

The paper layer this course asks for is four sentences — what made me curious, what the reader can do, what I noticed, what I would try next — and here they are, written twice for this very page. One draft spends evidence it doesn't have. Tap any rust-colored phrase to hear the charge against it.

How this page was made

Computed, live on this page: the soup, the settling, the census, the kind counts, the state hashes, claim 1's receipt, claim 6's verification, and your score. The field guide's canonical forms are generated at load by stepping and rotating the base patterns in code — no canonical string in this file was typed by hand, because hand-typed ground truth is how field guides learn to lie.

Authored by me: the eleven names the guide knows, the six claims and their verdicts, both drafts, the predict question, and every explanatory sentence — all in the block marked EDIT HERE in this page's script.

Checked before shipping: the detector ran against hand-built boards — a block, a blinker (both phases), a beehive, a boat, a toad, a beacon, a mid-flight glider, and one still life the guide doesn't know (a long boat), which correctly landed in “unnamed.” Open this page's console and run S8.selftest() to watch that check repeat.

Detection recipe: union of the two alternating phases → 8-connected components → each phase translated to the origin → the smallest of its eight rotations and reflections → an object matches if either of its phases matches any phase of a guide entry. The census's cells column always sums to the population readout — a built-in audit.

Accuracy note — four honest footnotes. (1) This board is finite and its edges absorb: a glider that would sail forever on the true infinite plane dies at my wall, so travelers are undercounted here — real census work runs on unbounded or enormous grids. (2) The field guide is small on purpose — eleven names. “Unnamed (rarer than my field guide)” means rarer than my guide, not rare in Life. (3) The 84.7% and 99.9% quoted below the census are Catagolue's percentages, from millions of random soups — this page cites them and does not measure them, and my little board's proportions come out different (small board, hungry edges, no travelers). (4) One page's soup is an anecdote. The census is the evidence.

What I would try next

Add a twelfth name — the eater — and count how many soups it takes to finally meet one. Add a torus switch, so gliders wrap instead of dying on my wall, and watch the census tilt toward travelers. And the one I really want: an “I found a stranger” button that freezes any unnamed object so the reader can name it themselves — a field guide that grows the way real field guides grew.

What I chose and how AI helped

AI helped with: the engine and detector code — double-buffer stepping, flood fill, canonical forms — plus generating every oscillator phase and every symmetry programmatically at load, and drafting verdict prose that I then rewrote.

I chose: names-off as the default state (that is the lesson), the eleven names the guide knows and the refusal to hand-type their canonical forms, the six claims — including planting claim 5 as a trap — the three bins and their exact wording, letting claim 6 be verified live instead of believed, and every sentence of both drafts.

The words are honest. Next: arrange them — order, labels, and the first ten seconds decide whether a stranger ever finds the door. That's the reader's path.
