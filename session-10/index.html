The Second Opinion

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. An instrument you never test is an opinion with a dashboard. So this week the study judges its judge: I handed my rubric and all twenty-four rows to an AI and let it score them cold, then set its verdicts against the human panel's, criterion by criterion. Where the two agree, my instrument gets stronger. Where they split, something better than agreement appears: a map of exactly what a machine can check — and what it still can't feel.

This week's question: when an AI judges my study with my own rubric, where does it agree with the human panel — and where does it quietly vote for the false mean?

Method

  • Panel scores: fetched unchanged from ../session-07/data/dataset.json — the same 24 analysis rows every page since Session 7 has used, flagged drift row excluded by the standing rule.
  • Judge scores: data/judge.json — one score per row per criterion, same rubric definitions the humans used.
  • Agreement metric: the share of rows where judge and panel land within one point of each other, per criterion — coarse on purpose; the limitation says what it hides.
  • Everything is computed in your browser at load, and every number in the prose is injected from that computation.

Interpretation

Both judges crown the true swerve — that much survives. But look at what the machine does to the shape of the result: the panel's lead of … points shrinks to …, and the false mean is promoted to a close second. On craft the two judges are nearly one mind — fit and form never land more than a point apart. On return, the criterion that asks whether you'd willingly read it again, they agree on only … of rows. The three hardest splits tell you why: the machine paid the lighthouse keeper's "restless sea" a return of 5 — polish, promoted; it scored the piano-tuner line the room almost crowned as noise; and it couldn't feel why a keeper whose whole job is to be looked at pays off on the tenth reading.

So the second opinion is in: the machine can verify craft; it cannot yet feel whether a break earned its keep. Asked to find excellence, it half-voted for its own reflection — and for my study that is better than agreement, because now I know which criteria my instrument can delegate and which ones stay human.

Limitation. The judge's scores are authored, illustrative stand-ins until the real run (their file says so above) — shaped to match documented judge biases, but shaped. One judge, one prompt, one room. "Within one point" is a blunt ruler that flatters small drifts. And when judge and panel disagree, nothing here proves the humans right: an eleven-person panel has blind spots too, and the machine calling our favorite row overrated would be a finding, not a malfunction.

Try it yourself

Hand your own rubric to an AI judge — your items, your definitions, scored blind through my tool (no keys, as always). Then compare before you trust either side. Every row where the machine splits from your raters is one honest sentence for your paper: name who split, by how much, and which criterion carried the disagreement. Compare, don't obey.

The study now has results, an argument, and a judge that's been judged. What it has never had is a hostile reader. Next week, that's you: the class gets to break my project — while I sit silently and take notes.
