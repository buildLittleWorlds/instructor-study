Fitted or Veneer?

Daniel — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. Attention lets a model read every word in the light of every other — so a stated audience should change everything about its answer. Sometimes it does. And sometimes the model re-dresses the same skeleton in new adjectives. This week's word is fit: quality that belongs to its purpose. The question is how deep the model's fit goes.

This week's question: when we hold a request constant and vary only the commission — who it's for, what it's for — does the model truly re-fit its answer, or just re-polish it?

      Method

      Variable — the commission clause: the single phrase naming who and what the description is for.

      Controls — everything else: the same fictional clock tower, the same request stem, one output per commission, same model, same settings, a fresh chat per run.

      Measures — (1) whether readers can match outputs to commissions blind; (2) readers' fitted-vs-veneer verdicts; (3) recurring phrases across all four outputs.

      This paragraph shape — one variable, named controls, stated measures — is the template for every method section in this course.

      Interpretation

      The adaptation is real: the registers differ enough that most readers match all four commissions on the first try. But run the bones detector and the skeleton shows through — the same sentimental joints (stood watch, generations) hold up four supposedly different bodies. And notice the journal entry: its dates, architect's materials, and restoration history sound authoritative and are entirely invented — the tower is fictional. Fluent authority is part of the veneer. Fit, it seems, is real but shallow by default: the model adjusts the surface it shows each audience faster than it changes what it actually says.

      Limitation. One object, one model, one output per commission — the recurring phrases could partly be coincidence at this sample size. "Fitted vs. veneer" is a fuzzy judgment with no rubric yet, so disagreement between raters here is expected and unmeasured. Making that judgment measurable is precisely where this study goes next.

      Try it yourself

      Hold one request constant in your own chat — a game tutorial, a recipe, a team recap — and vary only the audience: new player vs. veteran, beginner vs. chef, local fan vs. national reader. Then hunt for the phrase that survives every audience. That phrase is your model's veneer. Bring it to class.

      If quality depends on fit, is taste just personal preference — whatever each audience happens to like? (A design-industry CEO says no: taste is judgment experts can converge on. In Week 6, we test that claim on ourselves.)
