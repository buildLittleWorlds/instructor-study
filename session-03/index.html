Attention Spotlight

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. A modern model reads every word in the light of every other word — a mechanism called attention. It never looks up a fixed meaning for a word; it builds the meaning on the spot from the words nearby. That's why the same word can mean opposite things two sentences apart, and it's the reason context can change everything an answer says.

This week's question: can we actually watch a word decide what it means by looking at its neighbors?

Interpretation

Watch "bank" in the two sentences: it reaches for river and current in one, and savings and money in the other. The model didn't store two definitions and pick one — it grew the meaning fresh, each time, from whatever surrounded it. That is attention doing its job. And it's why the situation you hand a model — who the answer is for, what came before — really can reshape everything downstream. Meaning here is not a fixed label on a word; it's something computed live from the neighborhood.

Limitation. The glowing links are my illustration of which neighbors matter, not the exact numbers pulled from a real model's attention layers (a model spreads faint attention across every word, in many layers at once). The picture is true in shape — meaning is built from context — but it's a clean teaching diagram, not a raw readout.

Try it yourself

Find your own two-faced word — "light," "match," "wave," "cold," "bat" — and write two short sentences that force opposite meanings. Ask your AI chat to define the word in each; watch it never even mention the "wrong" meaning. The neighbors did all the work.

If meaning is built from context, then a model should reshape its whole answer when you change who it's for. Does it — or does it just re-decorate the same answer? (That's the judgment we start measuring next.)
