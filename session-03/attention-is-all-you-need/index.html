Attention Is All You Need

Imagine translating a sentence while being allowed to look at all of its words at once. In 2017, eight researchers built a new kind of language system around that simple idea.

You do not need a technical background.This tour starts with pictures and everyday language. The paper's specialist terms appear in smaller labels so you can recognize them if you open the original.

Ashish Vaswani and seven coauthors · shared in 2017 · published at NeurIPS 2017. This page stays with what that paper proposed and tested, before chatbots and today's large language models.

1
The 2017 problem

Why process a sentence one word at a time?

Paper §§1 and 4Table 1

Earlier language systems often read like a relay race: word one passed a message to word two, word two passed it to word three, and so on. Information from far-apart words had to travel through every handoff. The Transformer offered every word a direct line to every other word.

See the two reading stylesinteractive comparison
10

Relay reading

10 ordered handoffs

Each word must wait for the message from the word before it. The paper calls this a recurrent model.

All-at-once reading

1 direct look across the sentence

Every word can check every other word at the same time. The paper calls this self-attention.

Choose a length, then run the comparison.

The big idea: fewer handoffs make it easier to connect distant words, and many of those connections can be calculated at the same time during training.

What attention does

Which other words are useful right now?

Paper §3.2Equation 1Figure 2

For each word, the model looks across the sentence and gives more influence to words that seem useful. Think of a movable spotlight: it can shine brightly on one word and faintly on others.

Optional: see the paper's equation
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKT / √dk)V

In plain language: the model compares a question-like signal (Q) with label-like signals (K), turns the results into shares that add to 100%, then blends the information carried by V. Dividing by √dk keeps very large numbers from making the choices too extreme while the model learns.

Where these numbers came from: the word connection comes from the paper's Figure 3. We invented the four scores on this page to make the idea easy to see; they are not measurements from the authors' model.

Several spotlights at once

One sentence can have more than one useful pattern

Paper §3.2.2Figures 3–5

One spotlight can gather one useful kind of clue, but a word may need several kinds at once. The Transformer gives each word several spotlights—called attention heads—then combines the partial views they collect.

The heads do not vote on one winning line. The Transformer keeps information from each head and mixes it into an updated representation of the active word.

Why use more than one head? Different spotlights can collect different useful clues at the same time, giving every word a richer view of the sentence than one spotlight alone.

A useful caution, after the basic idea: these percentages and rays are simplified teaching data, not measurements copied from the paper. Real heads work with number patterns, not English questions; not every head has one neat job, and attention alone is not a complete explanation of model behavior.

4
The whole Transformer

Attention is the star, but it is not the whole machine

Paper §§3.1–3.5Figure 1

The title sounds as if the model contains only attention. It does not. Attention helps words exchange information; other parts keep track of word order, reshape the information, and build the translation.

Open the translation machinesimplified Figure 1

Reader encoder · repeated 6×

Turn words and their order into numbersembeddings + positional encoding
↓
Let each word look at the othersmulti-head self-attention

Keep and steady the resultresidual connection + layer normalization
The reader's job: build a rich understanding of the whole input sentence. It repeatedly lets words share clues, reshapes those clues, and preserves useful earlier information.

Writer decoder · repeated 6×

Turn words already written and their order into numbersoutput embeddings + positional encoding
↓
Look only at words already writtenmasked multi-head self-attention
Keep and steady that informationresidual connection + layer normalization
Look back at the input sentenceencoder–decoder attention
Keep and steady the resultresidual connection + layer normalization
Reshape what the next word should usefeed-forward network
Keep and steady the resultresidual connection + layer normalization
Choose the next wordlinear layer + softmax
The writer's job: produce the translation one word at a time. It can look at words it has already written and at the reader's understanding of the input, but it cannot peek at future words.
How do the two sides work together? The reader passes its understanding of the input to the writer. When choosing each translated word, the writer can spotlight the most useful parts of that understanding. Position signals act like numbered seats, so the model can tell which word came first, second, and so on.

The full Transformer reads the input, shares clues between words, remembers their order, and writes the output one word at a time.

What the paper demonstrated

Did the new approach actually work?

A clever diagram is not enough. The researchers compared the design with other approaches and tested it on two established translation challenges.

A shorter route is not free. Self-attention compares every word with every other word, so longer passages require many more comparisons.

28.4 English → German score

The paper's larger Transformer scored 28.4 BLEU, the best published result the authors compared against.

41.8 English → French score

The same model scored 41.8 BLEU on a second translation challenge.

3.5 days Time to train the large model

The authors report 3.5 days on one powerful machine using eight specialized processors called GPUs.

What is BLEU? It is a score that checks how much a machine translation overlaps with translations written by people. Higher was better on these 2014 tests. It is useful but imperfect: it does not capture every part of translation quality, and it is not a score for intelligence.

The paper showed that this new translation system could train efficiently and produce leading results on the specific 2017 tests—not that it understood language exactly as people do.

Read the page honestly

Four things this page does not claim

Our teaching numbers are not the paper's measurementsWe invented the interactive scores and line strengths. Results taken from the paper are clearly labeled.
Attention lines are not a model's complete explanationThey show which information received more weight, but many other calculations also shaped the answer.
Training together does not mean writing togetherDuring training, many known words can be processed at once. When producing new text, the paper's model still writes one word at a time.
This 2017 Transformer is not the same as every modern chatbotIt had a reader and a writer built for translation. Many later language models kept only a writer-like half and changed other details.
