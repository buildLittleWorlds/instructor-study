The Four Corners

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. An argument written in research language is a map a stranger can walk alone: name your axes, define your corners, and let the reader place the evidence themselves — before you tell them where the room put it. This page is my study's whole claim, redrawn as a map with four corners. Sort the cards. Then we compare.

This week's question: given only the words on the card — no scores, no labels — do readers put quality where my study says it lives? And when the room disagrees with the card's origin, which should I believe?

The map

Two questions locate any piece of writing on this map. Left to right: does it do the expected thing, or does it break an expectation? Bottom to top: do its choices serve the whole — does every part have a job — or is it just gesturing? Those two axes give four corners, and you already know their names.

Method

  • Items: six of the eight cards are rows from the study's run, fetched by id from ../session-07/data/dataset.json at load — their texts are never copied into this page, so the cards cannot drift from the evidence. Two cards are classic-form specimens I wrote myself, and the reveal says which.
  • The answer key is a room, not me: the consensus in data/consensus.json is the class's own sorting, collected with the copy-my-sorting button and tallied per card.
  • Order matters: the page lets you export your sorting before the reveal, because a prediction recorded after the answer is not a prediction.
  • Agreement is computed in your browser — your placements against the room's majority, card by card.

Interpretation

Watch what the room does to provenance. The card that came from the directed condition with the weakest score tends to land in classic form — the commission asked for a break, and the room couldn't find one, so the label "directed" earned it nothing. And the random condition's one dazzling card splits the room almost evenly between the random swerve and the true swerve — noise can wear purpose's clothes for one sentence at a time. That is why this study rates outputs blind and in bulk: where a card came from and where it belongs are different facts, and only the second one is about quality.

Notice also what this page is practicing: research language. A marketing sentence says "readers loved the true swerve." A research sentence says how many, out of how many, judged blind, disagreeing where — and then says what that cannot show. The reveal above gives you the second kind of sentence, every time.

Limitation. A consensus of one class is a room, not the world — eleven sorters can share a blind spot. Forced corners hide honest "in between" placements. The two classic-form cards were written by me, which means I planted the specimens I then asked the room to certify. And style is a leak: some cards may be sorted by their AI-flavored phrasing rather than their quality, which this design cannot fully separate.

Try it yourself

Take four outputs from your own study and name your own two axes — the two questions your claim actually turns on. Make a friend sort your outputs before they see your labels. Every card where they disagree with you is not an error: it is your next paragraph.

The room and I mostly agree about where quality lives on this map. Next week I hand the same job to a machine: my rubric, my items, an AI as the judge. Will it find the true swerve — or will it reward polish, and vote for its own reflection?
