The Argument You Run

Dr. Plate — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning

What made me curious

The genre my students are writing in is older than the web — it started with a magazine column readers could only finish by running it themselves, on graph paper, in 1970. I wanted one page where you get to be one of those readers for ten minutes, and then a lab for the question their successors spent fifty years learning to ask: what does the order of a page do to the stranger walking through it?

What to notice

A paper-app makes an idea traversable — not summarized, walked. A reader enters somewhere, does something, and comes out knowing the idea from the inside. Order, labels, and interaction are the page's whole grammar: they decide what a stranger meets first, what they are invited to do, and what the doing teaches. Change the order and you change the argument, even when every word stays the same.

The two modules below take that claim in sequence. The first runs the genre's founding argument the way its first readers ran it — by hand — and then walks the lineage from that magazine column to the museum pieces in our anthology. The second holds a page's content perfectly still and moves only its order, so you can watch the argument change while the words don't.

Course boundary: this page is an instructor demonstration. Both boards run and grade themselves in your browser; nothing is collected, exported, or submitted.

Be a 1970 reader

October 1970. Martin Gardner's "Mathematical Games" column in Scientific American introduces a game with no players, invented by the mathematician John Conway: one rule, applied to every square of a grid at once. The column could print the rule and a few snapshots. It could not print the thing itself.

What happened next founded the genre this course teaches. Readers didn't just read the column — they ran it: on graph paper and Go boards, on university mainframes after hours, and they mailed in what they found. The column's argument — that a rule this small can grow behavior this alive — was completable only by running it. You're about to join them.

The hand check

On the left, generation 1: the R-pentomino, the five-cell pattern that made Life famous — on a big enough board it churns for more than a thousand generations before it settles. Gardner's readers met it exactly like this: printed, inert, daring them to find out.

Session 9's bar

"A classmate can use the page without you narrating."

The sound-off test: hand someone your page, say nothing, and watch where their cursor stalls. Every stall is a card out of order — or a label doing less work than it thinks it is.

Order, labels, and interaction are doing the arguing — arrange them so a stranger knows where to start.

How this page was made

Computed: every board on this page is stepped live by the same twelve-line B3/S23 function — the answer keys for both hand-check rounds, the cell-by-cell grading against them, the blinker in the miniature, and the first-ten-seconds chip's reading of your current order. No answer key is typed anywhere in this file; reseed the left board and the page grades the new pattern just as strictly.

Authored: the curiosity, the framing paragraphs, the six lineage stops, every sentence on the five cards, and the three orders with their cost commentary. All of it is flagged EDIT HERE — the data blocks in the script, the prose in the HTML.

Accuracy note. The lineage is compressed: the dates and names are right, but the strip names eras, not every ancestor — there are decades of explorable work between Gardner and the web that six cards can't hold. The mini-blinker is the real B3/S23 rule on a bounded 5×5 board, where the bound never touches a centered blinker. The hand check grades against exact bounded-grid Conway, computed live on the 9×9 — and an infinite board would give the same answers for these generations: the R-pentomino's growth stays inside this frame through generation 12 and first pokes past it at generation 13, so generations 2 and 3 are safe by a wide margin. I checked all of this by simulation on larger and unbounded boards before writing it here; the check script is in the session README.

What I would try next

Add a round 3 — generation 3 → 4 — and then a run-ahead button to generation 13, where the pattern first touches this frame and a bounded board begins, politely, to lie: a reader who has been the computer should also feel where the computer's desk ends. And let each lineage card open its museum entry directly, so every stop on the strip ends in a door.

What I chose and how AI helped

AI helped with: drafting the page's code and both modules, wiring the grading and the reorderable preview, and running the scratch simulations that verify the boards (script recorded in this session's README).

I chose: the R-pentomino and the two-round structure of the hand check, the six stops on the lineage and what fifty years had to leave out, every sentence on the five cards, the three orders and the cost each one charges a stranger, and the decision to grade the reader's board like a computer's instead of replaying the answer — the ache is the lesson.

A stranger can walk the page now. One question left standing, and it's the oldest one here: who is the author of the thing that emerged? Next session.
