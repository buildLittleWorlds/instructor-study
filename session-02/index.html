The False Mean

Daniel — AI + Research, Level 2 · Youth Horizons Learning
Thesis. A language model's default output is the false mean: fluent, polished, and average. Turning up randomness (the "temperature" setting) buys surprise — but it pays for it in coherence. Difference is cheap. Quality is not.

This week's question: as the model gets more random, do readers find its writing more surprising — and where does the writing start falling apart?

Interpretation

Across raters so far, surprise climbs with temperature and coherence falls — two lines crossing. The writing that scores best overall sits in the middle: surprising enough to notice, coherent enough to trust. The extremes fail differently: low temperature gives near-twin answers (find the two cards that are almost the same sentence — that is the false mean showing itself), and high temperature gives dazzling fragments that stop meaning anything. More random is not more creative. It is just more random.

Limitation. One prompt, one model, two outputs per setting, and a small self-selected group of raters. Surprise and coherence are only two criteria of many — a real quality rubric arrives in Week 6. And note the exception that keeps this honest: occasionally a high-temperature card lands a genuinely great line. The curve is a tendency, not a law.

Try it yourself

Your chat app hides the temperature dial, but you can approximate the experiment: regenerate the same prompt five times and see how much actually changes. Then ask for “five answers, from safest to wildest” — is the wildest one better, or just noisier? Bring your best wild-but-good line to class; those exceptions are where the interesting questions live.

Randomness bought surprise and spent coherence — nothing here improved both at once. What could? (My bet, on the record in Week 5: a break made on purpose, for a reason. Not noise — judgment.)
