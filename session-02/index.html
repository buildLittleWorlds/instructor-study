The Garden of Forking Sentences

Thesis. A model doesn't "write" a sentence so much as walk down a tree, choosing a likely next word at every fork. A setting called temperature changes how boldly it strays from the safest branch. Turn it down and the tree becomes a single trunk — the same sentence every time. Turn it up and it explodes into a wild bush. Difference is easy to buy. Quality is the hard part.

This week's question: what does temperature actually do to the sentences a model can produce — and where does "more surprising" turn into "less coherent"?

The garden

Every path from the top down to a tip is one possible sentence beginning with "…". Thicker, darker branches are the likelier words. Drag the temperature dial and watch the tree change shape.

Interpretation

At a cold temperature the walk almost always takes the fattest branch at every fork, so you get the same sane, dull sentence again and again — the model's false mean. Crank the heat and the shy branches ("breathing," "underwater," "furious") start winning often enough to appear — some delightful, some nonsense. The count of distinct sentences climbs fast. But notice what climbing randomness does not do: it never invents a new branch. Every wild sentence was already in the tree; temperature only changes how often we visit the strange tips. More random is not more creative — it's just more willing to wander.

Limitation. This is a tiny, hand-built tree with made-up probabilities; a real model chooses from tens of thousands of words at every step and rebuilds the whole tree after each one. The shape of the lesson is right — cold repeats, hot scatters — but the numbers here are an illustration, not a measurement.

Try it yourself

In your own AI chat, run one prompt five times and count how much really changes (that's low temperature at work). Then ask for "five versions, from safest to wildest." Somewhere in the wild ones is a line that's surprising and good — that rare tip is what the rest of this course is hunting for.

Randomness gave us new sentences but never new ideas — every wild one was already on the tree. So where would a genuinely better sentence come from? (Hold that. It's the whole question.)
