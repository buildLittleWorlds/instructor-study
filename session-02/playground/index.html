Loading the Dice

A toy you can play with instead of a paragraph you can read
The claim, playable: a language model writes by rolling weighted dice over possible next words. Temperature doesn't add ideas — it only reshapes the weights. Drag the dial and watch: the distribution flattens or sharpens, but the same twelve words are all it ever had.

“The old library was ___”

temp T = 1.00

Three things to try, in order: drag the dial to 0.1 and roll 20 times (meet the false mean — the same word, almost every roll). Drag to 2.0 and roll 20 (everything possible, nothing chosen). Then hunt for the dial position where the rolls feel interestingly varied but still sane — and notice that you, the reader, just reinvented the quality curve from the main paper.

Method

Twelve candidate next words with hand-set base probabilities (roughly the shape a real model produces for this context: one dominant choice, a few plausible ones, a long tail). The dial applies the real temperature formula — each probability is raised to the power 1/T and renormalized — the same math running inside an actual model's sampler, just over 12 words instead of ~100,000.

Limitation. This is a toy: real models recompute the entire distribution after every word, conditioned on everything so far, and their tail is fifty thousand words deep. The toy shows the mechanism, not the scale — and nothing about the mechanism tells you which word is best. The dice know probabilities, not quality.

If the dial can't add ideas — only spread the dice — then where would better writing ever come from? (Back to the blind gallery, where readers judge exactly that.)
