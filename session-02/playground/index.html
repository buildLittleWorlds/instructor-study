Loading the Dice

A toy you can play with instead of a paragraph you can read
The claim, playable: after a language model computes next-token scores, a sampler can choose by rolling weighted dice. Temperature doesn't add ideas—it only reshapes the weights. Drag the dial and watch: the distribution flattens or sharpens, but the same twelve words are all it ever had.
This week’s question: When a system surprises us, did the surprise come from its input, its rule/learned parameters, or randomness while it ran?

Interpretation

Low temperature concentrates the weight on the safest words; high temperature gives the tail more chances to win. The possible words do not change. Temperature neither edits the learned model nor adds knowledge—it changes only the odds used when sampling the next token.

Callback: the dice are not the whole transformer

The transformer’s forward pass first computes next-token scores from continuous, high-dimensional token states. This toy begins only at the sampling step. A random starting board in Life is not temperature, and the deliberately noisy Life variant is not temperature either.

Method

Twelve candidate next words use hand-set illustrative base weights: one dominant choice, a few plausible ones, and a long tail. Rounded as written, they sum to 99.5%; the browser renormalizes them. The dial then applies the real temperature transformation—each weight is raised to the power 1/T and renormalized. Every displayed percentage and every roll is computed in-browser.

Limitation. This is a toy: real models recompute a distribution over a model-specific vocabulary after every token, conditioned on the available context. The toy shows the temperature mechanism, not the architecture, vocabulary scale, learned scores, or quality. The dice know probabilities, not which word is best.

Try it yourself

Drag the dial to 0.1 and roll 20 times. Clear, move to 2.0, and roll 20 again. Then hunt for the setting where the results feel varied but still plausible. Record the temperature, the number of distinct words, and one judgment the counter cannot make for you.

Temperature changes which token wins. But before the dice are rolled, how did every token help compute those weights? If one position can see everything in its context at once, what becomes possible? Next: Attention Spotlight, Session 3.
